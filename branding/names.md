# PrayerApp — naming candidates

The current name "PrayerApp" / "Prayer times" is descriptive but generic — it tells the store listing what the app does, not what it *feels* like. The five candidates below are rooted in Islamic vocabulary that almost any Muslim across language groups already recognises, and each one carries a visual identity we can lean on for the icon, the launch screen, and the time-of-day theming.

The brief: "pleasant, rich in features, magical in simplicity, and elegant in design." The app is opened in moments of intention; the brand should feel calm, reverent, and quietly Islamic without being heavy.

## 1. Mihrab — محراب

**The arched niche in the wall of every mosque that points toward Mecca.**

Why it fits: Mihrab is the literal architecture of prayer orientation. A user opens the app to know where Mecca is, when the next prayer is, what surah to read — Mihrab is *the* word for "the place that orients you toward Allah." It is also one of the most visually distinctive shapes in Islamic architecture: the pointed arch reads as Islamic from across the room, but it is not heavy or sectarian — Sunni, Shia, Sufi mosques all have a mihrab. As a name it is short, two syllables, easy to pronounce in any of the 13 supported locales, and is already a loanword in many of them.

Tone: reverent, architectural, elegant. The icon writes itself — a pointed-arch silhouette.

## 2. Sakinah — سكينة

**Divine tranquility / spiritual calm. A Quranic word: "He sent down sakinah upon them."**

Why it fits: Sakinah is the exact emotional state the app is trying to evoke. The CLAUDE.md design principles open with "Calm before clever" and "The app shouldn't shout" — sakinah is the Arabic word for that. It is feminine in form, soft in sound, and used in the Quran (48:4, 48:18, 48:26) for the peace God sends down on the believer. Unlike "Mihrab," which is architectural and external, "Sakinah" is internal — the state the user is in *while* using the app.

Tone: soft, reverent, contemplative. The icon should be quiet — a single point of light, a calm ripple.

## 3. Awqat — أوقات

**"The times." Plural of waqt. Used directly in Arabic for the five prayer times: awqat al-salah.**

Why it fits: Functional, immediately understood by any Arabic-speaking user — and recognisable as a loanword to most Muslim users in Urdu, Turkish, Indonesian, Malay, etc. The word does what the app does: it manages *the times*. It is also the only candidate on this list that doesn't need to be explained — a Muslim user opening the app store will read "Awqat" and know exactly what's inside. Shorter and more distinctive than "Prayer Times" without sacrificing clarity.

Tone: functional, grounded, Arabic-first. The icon can be temporal — five marks on a horizon arc representing the five daily prayers.

## 4. Hayya — حيّ

**"Come" — from the call to prayer: "Hayya 'ala-s-salah, hayya 'ala-l-falah" (come to prayer, come to success).**

Why it fits: Hayya is the verb that already gets shouted from every minaret on earth, five times a day. It is short (two syllables, four letters), kinetic, and *active* in a way none of the other candidates are. Where "Mihrab" is a place and "Sakinah" is a state, "Hayya" is an invitation. It also carries no sectarian weight and is the one word in the adhan that is grammatically directed at *you* — perfectly matched to a phone notification that says "it's time."

Tone: warm, active, inviting. The icon can be directional — a quiet arrow or a forward motion in a calligraphic stroke.

## 5. Hilal — هلال

**The new crescent moon. The sign that begins every Hijri month and confirms Ramadan, Eid, and the start of the Islamic calendar.**

Why it fits: Hilal is the most universally Islamic symbol in the world — the crescent on flags from Pakistan to Algeria, on the dome of every mosque, on the moonsighting committee certificates the app already integrates with. The word "hilal" specifically means the *new* crescent (not just any moon), which carries a sense of beginning, of orientation in time — exactly what a prayer-times app does. Short, recognisable, easy to pronounce. Risk: more visually crowded space (other apps already use crescent imagery), so the logo has to be unusually restrained to stand out.

Tone: classic, calendrical, immediately Islamic. The icon is the obvious one — the crescent itself, but stripped to its purest form.

---

## My recommendation

If you want one word that does the most work: **Mihrab**. It is reverent without being heavy, has the strongest visual identity (the arch), is short, and orientation-toward-Allah is the one mental model every other feature in the app sits inside (prayer times, qibla, quran, mosques near you, fasting calendar — they're all forms of orientation).

If you want a name that captures the *feeling* of using the app rather than the function: **Sakinah**. It is the design philosophy in one word.

If you want the safest, most functional, most immediately understood: **Awqat**.
